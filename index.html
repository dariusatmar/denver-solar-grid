The Revolution

Solar Costs Have Collapsed. They're Still Falling.

Since 1976, solar PV module costs have fallen from $100/watt to under $0.07/watt — a 1,400× reduction. The production-weighted learning rate is 48%: for every doubling of cumulative global production, costs drop by nearly half. Module costs are falling up to 20% per year — twice what they were five years ago.

This isn't a government subsidy story. It's a manufacturing learning curve — the same force that made computers, LEDs, and lithium batteries cheap. Every factory built, every panel deployed makes the next one cheaper. And we're nowhere near the floor.

"Solar panels are a revolutionary technology — inert glass rectangles that, placed on the ground, print out wealth at roughly 100x the rate of the best farm land." — Casey Handmer, Terraform Industries

Handmer's analysis shows that at near-future baseline costs of $100/kW for solar and $100/kWh for batteries, solar+storage is already cheaper than any other power source for nearly all locations and nearly all load types — replacing the concept of an "expensive green premium" with a green dividend.

What does this mean for Denver? A solar farm built today on Colorado's Eastern Plains generates electricity cheaper than the natural gas plants currently powering Denver's grid. Not in the future. Right now.

Why Denver beats Miami: Solar panels lose ~0.4% efficiency per °C above 25°C. Phoenix hits 115°F regularly. Miami is hot and humid year-round. Denver's cool, dry air means the panels run closer to their rated efficiency. The data proves it — Denver produces 1,831 MWh/MW/year vs Miami's 1,822 MWh/MW/year on identical systems.
🏔️

Mile-High Irradiance

At 5,280 ft (1,604m per NREL station data), Denver sits above ~15% of Earth's atmosphere. Less air means less light scatter, more direct beam energy on every panel.

🏜️

Bone-Dry Air

Denver's semi-arid climate means minimal humidity and haze. Water vapor absorbs and scatters sunlight — Denver has almost none. GHI of 4.83 kWh/m²/day, but the DNI/GHI ratio of 1.27 shows exceptional atmospheric clarity.

❄️

Cold Panels, Hot Output

Denver's cool temperatures boost panel efficiency. This isn't theoretical — PVWatts proves it: Denver produces more MWh per installed MW than Miami or Dallas despite lower GHI.

🌾

The Eastern Plains

The Problem

Colorado's Grid Is Dirty, Expensive, and Falling Behind

Colorado's electricity grid remains 63% fossil fuels. Natural gas is the primary energy source. The state emits 958 lbs of CO₂ per MWh — the 16th worst carbon intensity in the nation. And demand is about to surge.

Demand is about to explode

Three forces are converging on Denver's grid simultaneously:

🖥️

Datacenter Boom

Colorado's Front Range is a fast-growing datacenter market. AI demand is outstripping grid capacity nationwide. Hyperscalers need gigawatts, not megawatts.

🚗

EV Adoption

Colorado has one of the fastest EV adoption rates in the country. Every EV added is ~3,500 kWh/year of new demand. Multiply by hundreds of thousands of vehicles.

🏗️

Population Growth

The Front Range corridor continues to grow. More people, more homes, more cooling load as summers get hotter.

🔥

Electrification

Heat pumps replacing gas furnaces. Induction replacing gas stoves. Every appliance that switches from gas to electric adds grid load.

The policy mandate is set: Colorado's SB21-264 requires an 80% GHG reduction by 2030 for the electric sector. Xcel Energy has committed to 80% clean by 2030. The question isn't whether Colorado decarbonizes — it's how fast and who benefits.

The Missing Piece

Storage Changes Everything. Again.

"But what about when the sun doesn't shine?" Battery costs have fallen even faster than solar — down to $94/kWh at the pack level. Grid-scale storage makes solar fully dispatchable, and Handmer's analysis shows the economics are already decisive.

"Batteries and transmission are in direct competition. Both enable electricity arbitrage. And while batteries have a fixed cost per MWh delivered that is falling ~10% per year, transmission lines get more expensive as they get longer. The outcome is not in doubt." — Casey Handmer

Handmer's detailed simulations — using real-world 5-minute-interval NSRDB solar data — show that at $100/kW for solar and $100/kWh for batteries, the optimal utilization for even expensive loads like datacenters reaches 99.9% uptime. For lower-capex industrial loads that can tolerate intermittency, the economics become almost absurdly favorable.

What storage unlocks for Denver's grid

⚡

Kill the Gas Peakers

Gas peaker plants charge $115–175/MWh and run only at peak demand. A 4-hour battery charged by $30/MWh solar replaces them — at a fraction of the cost, zero emissions, and millisecond response time.

🌙

Solar After Dark

4-hour batteries shift afternoon solar surplus into the evening demand peak. Denver's summer solar surplus is massive — bank it for after sunset.

🔋

Distributed Power

Batteries at solar farms, at substations, behind every meter. As Handmer writes: "Market power will shift from monopoly utilities to confederations of illegible behind-the-meter demand."

🛡️

Grid Resilience

Batteries respond to frequency changes in milliseconds — faster than any turbine. They provide ancillary services better than the generators they replace.

The Winter Question — Solved

"Storing electricity for months is economically difficult, but storing heat is easy." — Casey Handmer

Standard Thermal is building ultra-low-cost thermal storage — giant insulated sand batteries heated by excess summer solar. When winter comes, the heat is extracted. Cheap, scalable, and self-insulating. Artificial geothermal powered by summer sun.

And Denver's winter solar "problem" is overstated. NREL data shows December DNI in Denver (4.94 kWh/m²/day) is higher than Chicago's annual average (3.72) and Seattle's best month. Denver's worst is better than most cities' best.

The Opportunity

Datacenters Will Build Solar Whether Denver Does or Not

The biggest electricity consumers of the 2020s — AI datacenters — are already building captive solar plants. The question for Denver isn't whether to compete for grid power. It's whether to be the place they build.

"Datacenters are already building their own captive power plants. As AI demand outstrips production of gas turbines, hyperscalers will turn to offgrid solar+battery power systems, which are already competitive with pure gas or gas+solar in the sunnier parts of Earth." — Casey Handmer, "Energy Predictions 2025"

Handmer's analysis shows that for a datacenter load at ~$50,000/kW capex, a 10× solar overbuild plus batteries achieves >99.5% uptime — more reliable than grid power in most of the US. And here's the kicker:

Datacenters Become Net Power Sources

📊

10× Solar Overbuild

A datacenter needing 100 MW constant builds a ~1 GW solar array with batteries. Hits >99.5% uptime for GPUs — better than most grid connections.

⚡

~75% Curtailment = Free Power

That 1 GW array curtails roughly 75% of generated power most of the time. Hundreds of MW of clean electricity looking for a home.

🏘️

Community Power Source

The datacenter sells excess to the local grid at rock-bottom rates. The community gets cheap, clean power 99% of the time.

🔄

Market Power Flips

"Within five years, market power between utilities and datacenters will flip, with DCs becoming the preferred load growth power generation partner." — Handmer

The pitch to hyperscalers: Build on Colorado's Eastern Plains. 6.12 DNI, flat land at $2,000/acre, 30 miles from Denver's fiber backbone, a state legislating toward 80% clean electricity. You don't need the grid. You become the grid.
Beyond the kWh

Conquering the Primary Energy Layer

If solar only made cheap electricity, it would already be revolutionary. But Handmer's most important insight is bigger: cheap solar power can be converted into everything we currently drill, mine, and burn. This isn't about kilowatt-hours. It's about recompiling the foundation of industrial civilization.

"Three years ago we set out to make cheap synthetic natural gas from sunlight and air. At the time I didn't fully appreciate that we had kicked off the process of recompiling the foundation layer of our entire industrial stack." — Casey Handmer, "To Conquer the Primary Energy Consumption Layer"

Handmer's Terraform Industries analysis reveals a critical bifurcation in the economics of solar-powered industry. Their simulations using real NSRDB data at 5-minute intervals show two optimal operating modes:

🏭

Low-Capex Intermittent Loads (<$400/kW)

Run at <30% utilization, no batteries needed. Diurnal operation matches solar output directly. ~5× bigger green dividend than battery-backed loads. This is where the revolution happens.

🖥️

High-Capex Continuous Loads (>$2,000/kW)

Battery-backed, >75% utilization. Datacenters, smelters. Still economically favorable vs. grid power in sunny locations.

The sweet spot — low-capex, intermittency-friendly, solar-matched industrial loads — unlocks an entirely new category of manufacturing. What can you build right next to a solar array that turns on when the sun shines and off when it doesn't?

"Within the next five years, solar cost reductions will drive our process to be cost-preferred in all hydrocarbon import markets, and geological sources of oil and gas will never again be able to compete. Our grandchildren will be swimming in copious cheap energy and wondering what all that drilling was for." — Casey Handmer

For Colorado, this means something profound: the state's sunshine becomes an exportable commodity. Not electrons on a wire — synthetic fuels, chemicals, steel, cement, and materials produced from solar power, air, water, and common rocks. Colorado could become a net energy and materials exporter by farming the sun on the Eastern Plains.

The recipe: Generic raw inputs (sunlight, air, water, rocks) → adapt mature industrial processes to consume 2–3× more electricity at lower capex → make them intermittency-friendly → miniaturize to 1 MW scale adjacent to the array → output valuable, storable commodity products. Denver has the sunlight. The Eastern Plains have the land. The Front Range has the workforce.

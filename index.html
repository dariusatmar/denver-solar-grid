The Revolution

Solar Costs Have Collapsed. They're Still Falling.

Since 1976, solar PV module costs have fallen from $100/watt to under $0.07/watt — a 1,400x reduction. The production-weighted learning rate is 48%: for every doubling of cumulative global production, costs drop by nearly half. This isn't slowing down. It's accelerating.

This isn't a government subsidy story. It's a manufacturing learning curve — the same force that made computers, LEDs, and lithium batteries cheap. Every factory built, every panel deployed, every year of accumulated production makes the next panel cheaper. And we're nowhere near the floor.

"Solar panels are a revolutionary technology — inert glass rectangles that, placed on the ground, print out wealth at roughly 100x the rate of the best farm land." — Casey Handmer, Terraform Industries

At the grid scale, this cost collapse changes everything. Utility-scale solar — with single-axis tracking on cheap land — now produces electricity at $24–31/MWh unsubsidized. That's cheaper than operating most existing coal and gas plants, before you even count construction costs.

What does this mean for Denver? It means a solar farm built today on Colorado's Eastern Plains generates electricity cheaper than the natural gas plants currently powering Denver's grid. Not in the future. Right now.
Denver's Edge

🏔️

Mile-High Irradiance

At 5,280 ft, Denver sits above ~15% of Earth's atmosphere. Less air = less light scatter = more direct beam energy hitting every panel. NREL data confirms: Denver's DNI beats Miami, LA, and most US cities.

🏜️

Bone-Dry Air

Denver's semi-arid climate means minimal humidity and haze. Water vapor in the atmosphere absorbs and scatters sunlight — Denver has almost none. Solar panels perform best in exactly these conditions.

❄️

Cold Panels, Hot Output

Solar panels lose ~0.4% efficiency per °C above 25°C. Phoenix hits 115°F regularly — Denver rarely exceeds 95°F. Cooler panels produce more electricity per unit of sunlight received.

🌾

The Eastern Plains

Just 30 miles east of Denver: thousands of acres of flat, cheap agricultural land at $1,500–3,000/acre. Perfect for utility-scale solar arrays. Close enough to the Front Range load centers to minimize transmission costs.

"But isn't Denver too far north for solar?" — Denver is at 39.7°N, the same latitude as Madrid, Spain and Beijing, China. Latitude matters far less than cloud cover and atmospheric clarity. Denver has both clarity and 300 days of sunshine per year.

The Problem

Colorado's Grid Is Dirty, Expensive, and Falling Behind

Colorado's electricity grid remains 63% fossil fuels. Natural gas is the primary energy source. The state emits 958 lbs of CO₂ per MWh generated — the 16th worst carbon intensity in the nation. And demand is about to surge.

Demand is about to explode

Three forces are converging on Denver's grid simultaneously:

🖥️

Datacenter Boom

Colorado's Front Range is one of the fastest-growing datacenter markets in the US. AI inference and training demand is outstripping grid capacity. Hyperscalers need gigawatts, not megawatts.

🚗

EV Adoption

Colorado has one of the fastest EV adoption rates in the country. Every EV added to the road is another ~3,500 kWh/year of electricity demand. Multiply by hundreds of thousands of vehicles.

🏗️

Population Growth

The Front Range corridor (Denver–Boulder–Fort Collins–Colorado Springs) continues to grow. More people = more homes, businesses, and cooling load as summers get hotter.

🔥

Electrification

Heat pumps replacing gas furnaces. Induction replacing gas stoves. Every appliance that switches from gas to electric adds grid load. The grid needs to grow — and it needs to grow clean.

The policy mandate is already set: Colorado's SB21-264 requires an 80% GHG reduction by 2030 for the electric sector. Xcel Energy has committed to 80% clean electricity by 2030. The question isn't whether Colorado decarbonizes its grid — it's how fast and who benefits.

What storage unlocks for Denver's grid

⚡

Kill the Gas Peakers

Gas peaker plants run only during peak demand and charge $115–175/MWh. A 4-hour battery charged by $24/MWh solar replaces them entirely — at a fraction of the cost and zero emissions.

🌙

Solar After Dark

4-hour duration batteries shift afternoon solar generation into the evening peak. Solar + storage becomes a 24-hour power source. Colorado's evening demand peak is perfectly matched by afternoon solar surplus.

🔋

Grid Stability

Batteries respond to grid frequency changes in milliseconds — faster than any turbine. They provide frequency regulation, voltage support, and spinning reserves better than the generators they replace.

🏠

Distributed + Centralized

Batteries at the solar farm, at substations, and behind every meter. As Handmer notes: "Market power will shift from monopoly utilities to confederations of illegible behind-the-meter demand in the form of networked batteries."

The Winter Question — Solved

Seasonal storage remains the hardest problem. But Handmer identifies the key insight:

"Storing electricity for months is economically difficult, but storing heat is easy." — Casey Handmer, referencing Standard Thermal

Standard Thermal is building ultra-low-cost thermal energy storage — essentially giant insulated sand batteries heated by excess summer solar. When winter comes, the heat is extracted. The storage medium is cheap, infinitely scalable, and self-insulating. Think of it as artificial geothermal powered by summer sun.

For Denver specifically: March is actually the highest solar production month (cool temps + rising sun angle + snow reflection). Even December — the worst month — produces 79% of peak output on a properly tilted array. The seasonal swing is far smaller than most people assume.

The Opportunity

Datacenters Will Build Solar Whether Denver Does or Not

Here's the plot twist: the biggest consumers of electricity in the 2020s — AI datacenters — are already building their own captive solar plants. The question for Denver isn't whether to compete with datacenters for grid power. It's whether to be the place they build.

"Datacenters are already building their own captive power plants. As AI demand outstrips production of gas turbines, hyperscalers will turn to offgrid solar+battery power systems, which are already competitive with pure gas or gas+solar in the sunnier parts of Earth." — Casey Handmer

Handmer's most provocative prediction: datacenters will become net power sources for their communities.

The Datacenter Math

📊

10x Solar Overbuild

A datacenter that needs 100 MW constant builds a 1 GW solar array with batteries. This hits >99.5% uptime for GPUs — more reliable than grid power in most of the US.

⚡

75% Curtailment = Free Power

That 1 GW array is curtailing roughly 75% of its generated power most of the time. That's hundreds of MW of clean electricity looking for a home.

🏘️

Net Power Source

The datacenter sells its excess to the local grid at extremely competitive rates. The community gets access to cheap, clean power 99% of the time.

🔄

Power Dynamics Flip

"Within five years, market power between utilities and datacenters will flip, with DCs becoming the preferred load growth power generation partner." — Handmer

Denver's Front Range already hosts a growing cluster of datacenters. With its exceptional solar resource, cheap Eastern Plains land, and proximity to fiber backbone, Denver is a natural home for the next generation of solar-powered AI infrastructure.

The pitch to hyperscalers is simple: Build your next datacenter campus on Colorado's Eastern Plains. 6.12 DNI, flat land at $2,000/acre, <30 miles from Denver's fiber hubs, and a state government that's actively legislating toward 80% clean electricity. You don't need the grid. You become the grid.
Beyond the kWh

Solar Power Becomes Chemical Feedstock

If all solar did was make electricity, it would already be revolutionary. But Handmer's most important insight is bigger: cheap solar power can be converted into everything we currently drill, mine, and burn.

"Within the next five years, solar cost reductions will drive our process to be cost-preferred in all hydrocarbon import markets, and geological sources of oil and gas will never again be able to compete. Our grandchildren will be swimming in copious cheap energy and wondering what all that drilling was for." — Casey Handmer, Terraform Industries

The Terraform Process

Terraform Industries is pioneering the conversion of cheap solar power, air, and water into synthetic natural gas and other hydrocarbons:

☀️

Cheap Solar Electricity

At $24/MWh and falling, solar provides the energy input.

💧

Electrolysis → Hydrogen

Split water into hydrogen and oxygen using solar electricity.

🌬️

CO₂ Capture from Air

Lime-calcite process captures CO₂ directly from the atmosphere.

⛽

Synthetic Fuels

Combine H₂ + CO₂ → methane (CH₄), methanol (CH₃OH), and from methanol: DME, ethylene, propane, gasoline, kerosene, plastics — anything.

For Colorado, this means something profound: the state's sunshine becomes an exportable commodity. Not just electrons on a wire, but synthetic fuels, chemicals, and materials produced from solar power, air, and water. Colorado could become a net energy exporter — not by drilling, but by farming the sun.

✈️

Aviation Fuel

Kerosene synthesized from solar power, air, and water. Carbon-neutral aviation. DIA becomes a hub for sustainable fuel.

🏭

Industrial Chemicals

Methanol is the universal precursor for plastics, paints, fertilizers, adhesives. Solar-powered chemical production on the Front Range.
